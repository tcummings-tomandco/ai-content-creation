What can UK law firms use AI for in 2026?

UK law firms can use AI for document drafting, legal research support, contract review, court bundle preparation, billing, and routine client communication. The SRA has not banned any of these tools, but requires the responsible solicitor to verify every output, maintain client confidentiality, and stay competent under Paragraphs 3.2 and 3.3 of the SRA Code of Conduct. Two AI-first firms (Garfield.Law and LawFairy) received SRA authorisation in 2025 and 2026 for narrow, standardised practice areas.

Which AI tasks are considered lower-risk by the SRA?

The SRA does not publish an approved list of AI tools or use cases. Its approach is to apply existing professional obligations to whatever technology a firm chooses to use. In practice, risk tracks directly to how far the AI output reaches a client or a court before a qualified lawyer has reviewed it.

Administrative and internal tasks sit at the lower end of the risk spectrum. Using an AI assistant to transcribe attendance notes, generate first drafts of standard letters, summarise long documents, or search a firm's internal knowledge base carries relatively low regulatory risk, provided the output is reviewed before use and client-sensitive data is not sent to a third-party system without an appropriate data processing agreement.

Legal research sits higher on the spectrum, not because AI cannot find relevant material, but because it can confidently fabricate material that does not exist. Contract review and due diligence AI tools occupy the middle ground: useful for flagging potential issues, but not reliable as the sole review.

What does the duty of competence actually require when using AI?

Two paragraphs of the SRA Code of Conduct are central here. Paragraph 3.2 requires solicitors to ensure the service they provide is competent and delivered in a timely manner. Paragraph 3.3 requires them to maintain their competence and keep their professional knowledge and skills up to date, including in relation to the tools they use.

The SRA's compliance tips, updated 9 February 2026, make the practical implication explicit. A solicitor who uses an AI tool must understand how it works well enough to review its output critically, and must be able to recognise when the output may be wrong. Professional responsibility cannot be passed to the technology.

Specifically, a competent AI user in a legal context needs to understand three failure modes. Hallucination (fabricated citations, invented facts, non-existent statutes) is the most dangerous. Recency gaps matter too: a model trained on data before a legal change will not know about it. Prompt sensitivity is the third: slightly different phrasing can produce substantially different, and sometimes contradictory, answers.

Understanding those failure modes needs to be paired with a mandatory verification step. Before any AI-generated content reaches a client or a court, a qualified solicitor must have checked it. This is not optional good practice; Paragraph 3.2 makes it a regulatory requirement.

"Using AI does not dilute professional responsibility. If AI produces incorrect research or a defective draft and a solicitor relies on it without adequate verification, the solicitor is accountable." (SRA compliance tips, updated 9 February 2026)

What happened when solicitors submitted AI-hallucinated cases to the High Court?

On 6 June 2025, Dame Victoria Sharp P handed down judgment in the joined cases of Ayinde v London Borough of Haringey and Al-Haroun v Qatar National Bank [2025] EWHC 1383 (Admin). Both cases were referred under the Hamid Jurisdiction, a procedure for addressing serious failures in professional conduct in the preparation of legal proceedings.

The Al-Haroun figures are the ones that matter most for legal AI practice. The solicitors had cited 45 cases in support of their client's claim for damages. When the opposing side checked them, 18 did not exist. The materials had been generated by a large language model and submitted to court without anyone verifying whether the citations were real.

In Ayinde, five of the cases cited in a judicial review application also did not exist, and the grounds misidentified the effect of relevant legislation. The court found that "a language model such as ChatGPT is not capable of conducting reliable legal research" and may produce "well-formatted citations to cases, statutes, or guidance that simply do not exist."

Wasted costs orders were granted. The solicitors and barrister involved were referred to the SRA and Bar Standards Board respectively. The judge noted that the court's relative leniency on the day was not to be taken as precedent: lawyers who do not meet their obligations in this area "risk severe sanction." The failure in both cases was not using AI to assist with research. It was submitting its output without checking whether the cited cases existed.

How do the SRA's governance requirements apply to a small law firm?

The SRA's February 2026 compliance tips set out its minimum expectations when a firm introduces AI tools. The Compliance Officer for Legal Practice (COLP) takes primary responsibility for ensuring the firm's AI use meets its regulatory obligations. The COLP does not need to be an AI specialist, but does need to know which tools are in use, by whom, and on what data.

For a firm of three to ten fee-earners, the framework translates into three practical components.

A documented AI policy

One page is enough for a small firm. The policy should cover which AI tools are permitted, what data staff may and may not input into them, and the verification steps required before any AI output is used with or for a client. It does not need to read like a contract. It needs to exist, be communicated to all fee-earners, and be followed consistently.

Staff training

SRA Code Paragraph 3.3 requires staff to keep their professional knowledge current. For AI that means fee-earners need to understand the failure modes of any tool they use professionally. A 90-minute internal session covering hallucination, data protection basics, and the firm's verification protocol is a practical baseline. The Law Society's generative AI essentials practice note provides solid source material for anyone building that session.

Ongoing monitoring

The SRA expects firms to identify and manage material risks as they arise. For AI that means reviewing your tools when vendors update their systems significantly, when new regulatory guidance is published, or when a staff member flags a quality concern. For most small firms, a quarterly check within the COLP's existing compliance review is enough. You don't need a separate audit function for this.

None of this requires specialist AI legal counsel or expensive technology platforms. It requires the same methodical approach a competent COLP already brings to PII renewal, anti-money laundering checks, and data protection compliance.

Do law firms need to tell clients they are using AI?

There is no blanket SRA rule requiring disclosure every time a solicitor uses an AI drafting tool. The SRA's February 2026 guidance says firms should "make clear to clients where they are interfacing with AI," which is more specific than an obligation to disclose every tool in the drafting workflow.

The disclosure obligation becomes concrete when the client is directly interacting with an AI system (an automated intake chatbot, for instance) or when an AI-generated document is sent without a solicitor having reviewed it. A first-draft engagement letter that a fee-earner has edited, checked, and approved is a different situation from a letter the AI produced and nobody read before it left the office.

The practical approach is a short, plain-language paragraph in the client care letter. It should explain that the firm uses AI tools to support document preparation, that qualified solicitors review all AI-assisted work before it is sent, and that client data is only processed in systems with appropriate data protection agreements in place. That covers the disclosure obligation without alarming clients who have no particular concern about the technology.

What the SRA does require, clearly and without caveats: client data must stay confidential. Entering client-identifiable information into a public AI tool (the free tier of ChatGPT, for instance) without a data processing agreement in place is a potential breach of confidentiality under UK GDPR. That obligation applies regardless of whether you have disclosed to the client that you are using AI.

What do Garfield.Law and LawFairy tell us about AI in legal services?

Two SRA-authorised AI-first firms are now operating in England and Wales. They are worth examining because they show both the regulator's appetite for innovation and the limits it has drawn around it.

Garfield.Law received SRA authorisation in spring 2025, making it the first purely AI-driven firm authorised to provide regulated legal services in England and Wales. Its focus is small business debt recovery, guiding claimants through the small claims court process for debts up to £10,000. The system handles a standardised, step-by-step process rather than open-ended legal advice, and it automates the procedural pathway rather than exercising legal judgment.

LawFairy received SRA authorisation in February 2026 and focuses on immigration law, analysing visa eligibility, sponsorship, and citizenship routes. A key technical distinction from Garfield is that LawFairy uses deterministic AI (the law is encoded into structured decision pathways rather than generated by a language model). Deterministic systems produce consistent, auditable outputs: ask the same question twice and you get the same answer.

The SRA has been explicit that authorising these firms does not endorse AI as a replacement for lawyers in complex or judgment-intensive work. The common thread in both authorisations is that the AI handles narrow, well-defined, process-driven tasks where every output can be fully audited and where the risk of a plausible-but-wrong answer causing serious harm is substantially lower than in contested litigation or complex transactions.

For traditional law firms, the more interesting question Garfield.Law and LawFairy raise is not whether AI can automate a debt claim. It is which parts of your own practice area are, in effect, standardised process work, and whether a solicitor's time is best spent on those or on the judgment-intensive work that AI genuinely cannot replicate yet. That is a question worth answering before someone else answers it for you.

Where should a UK law firm start with AI?

Given the SRA's published guidance and the cases that have already gone wrong, a sensible starting sequence for a small or mid-size UK law firm looks like this.

1. Map what your staff are already using. Before buying anything new, find out which AI tools fee-earners are already using informally or privately for client work. Uncontrolled use is both a data protection risk and a professional conduct risk. The COLP's first job is visibility into the current state.

2. Pick one low-risk internal use case first. Attendance note transcription or first-draft standard letters are reliable starting points. Neither involves client-facing output delivered without human review. Run a three-month pilot, document the verification process, collect feedback from fee-earners, and expand only once that baseline is established and documented.

3. Sort the data protection baseline before using client data. Before any AI tool handles client-identifiable information, confirm the vendor has a Data Processing Agreement available, check where data is processed and stored, and conduct a Data Protection Impact Assessment. The ICO's AI and data protection guidance is the appropriate starting point, not a vendor's marketing FAQ.

4. Add legal research tools only with a mandatory verification protocol. If the firm wants AI-assisted case law or statutory research, build the verification step into the workflow as a required sign-off, not a suggested best practice. Before any AI-generated citation appears in a document going to a client or a court, a qualified solicitor must have checked it against the original source. The Ayinde and Al-Haroun cases make plain why this matters.

5. Update your client care letter. Add a short paragraph explaining the firm's AI use, the human oversight applied to all AI-assisted work, and how client data is handled. The Law Society's updated model client care letter includes a suggested placeholder for this purpose.

Tom & Co analysis of the SRA's Risk Outlook Report (November 2023) finds that the UK's largest solicitors' firms adopted AI at approximately 12 percentage points per year between 2020 and 2023: up from roughly 38% to 75% in three years. That was before ChatGPT became widely available in late 2022. The pace of adoption since has made governance a more pressing priority for the whole profession, not just the top-50 firms.

The firms that will get the most from AI are not the fastest adopters. They will be the ones with the smallest gap between what their tools produce and what a responsible solicitor has verified. Getting the governance right at the start is considerably cheaper than explaining to the SRA why it went wrong.
