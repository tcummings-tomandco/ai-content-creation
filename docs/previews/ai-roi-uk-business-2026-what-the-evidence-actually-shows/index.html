What is the ROI of AI in business in 2026? An honest UK evidence review

Last reviewed: 2026-04-29

AI return on investment in 2026 is real but concentrated. Around 6% of organisations report enterprise-wide EBIT impact of 5% or more from AI, while about 60% see no enterprise impact at all (McKinsey 2025). UK adoption has reached 54% of firms, up from 23% in 2023, with current users expecting +71 percentage points of net productivity gain over the next year (BCC March 2026). Function-level cost reductions cluster at 10 to 20% in software engineering, IT and manufacturing.

What does AI ROI mean for a UK business in 2026?

AI return on investment is not a single number. The published evidence falls into three categories that should not be added together.

Function-level ROI is cost or time saved on a specific workflow: contact-centre handling time, code review throughput, marketing brief turnaround. Easiest to measure, easiest to win, and the strongest published figures sit here.

Enterprise-level ROI is earnings before interest and tax (EBIT) impact at the company level, attributed back to AI use. Far harder. McKinsey's State of AI 2025 found that only 6% of respondents (the AI high-performers) report 5% or more EBIT impact attributable to AI. Roughly 60% see none.

Hidden ROI is the third category: avoided costs, retention improvements, decision-quality lifts. Real but speculative. Treat as a tie-breaker between options, not as a primary justification.

Most UK SMEs are conflating these in their internal cases. The classic mistake is to model an enterprise EBIT lift from a function-level proof point. The function-level wins are usually genuine. Scaling them to enterprise impact is where the published failure rate lives.

What returns are UK businesses actually seeing?

The honest answer in 2026 is that UK adoption has accelerated faster than UK measurement. The most recent BCC and University of Essex study, surveying 668 UK firms in March 2026, shows 54% of UK firms now use AI, up from 23% in 2023, 25% in 2024 and 35% in 2025. That is a near-tripling in three years.

The productivity expectation gap is the more interesting finding. Current AI users expect a net +71 percentage point productivity gain over the next twelve months. Firms still planning to adopt expect +46pp. The unsure expect +26pp. Non-adopters expect -3pp. The expectation gap between current users and non-adopters is statistically significant in the BCC dataset (p<0.001).

Two things are uncomfortable to read together. First, UK adoption is now mainstream. Second, enterprise EBIT impact is rare. The honest reading is that most UK firms are deriving genuine function-level value from AI but have not (yet) restructured around it enough to register at the company-financials level. McKinsey's working ratio is that for every £1 spent on AI technology, £5 should go to the people side: training, redesign, change management. Most UK SMEs are nowhere near that ratio.

The BCC data also shows a meaningful split between firms using bespoke AI (custom integrations, RAG systems, fine-tuned models) and those using only generic tools (per-seat ChatGPT or Copilot subscriptions). Bespoke users were nearly seven times more likely to report a workforce decrease over the past twelve months: 21% of the 61 firms using bespoke AI in the BCC sample, against 3% of the 265 firms using only generic tools (Fisher's exact p<0.001).

The implication is straightforward. Generic productivity AI distributes time savings broadly, which rarely shows up as headcount change. Bespoke AI deployed against a specific high-volume workflow can substitute for staff time, which sometimes does. Whether that is a virtue or a risk is a leadership choice; both reflect real ROI, just measured differently.

How much does AI cost a UK SME?

Sterling cost ranges for UK businesses, based on published vendor pricing as of April 2026.

  • Per-seat productivity AI: £18 to £50 per user per month for ChatGPT Pro, Claude Pro and Microsoft 365 Copilot. The published list price for Microsoft 365 Copilot for business is £24.70 per user per month. Negotiated enterprise tiers can run higher.
  • Customer-service or knowledge-base bots using off-the-shelf platforms: £200 to £2,000 per month plus implementation. Implementation typically £5,000 to £40,000 depending on integrations.
  • Bespoke retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems on private data: £25,000 to £200,000 to design and deploy, plus ongoing model and infrastructure costs of £500 to £5,000 per month.
  • Custom AI agent or fine-tuned model: £50,000 to £500,000 to build, with operating costs depending on usage volumes.
  • People and change-management spend: McKinsey's published guidance is roughly £5 of people investment for every £1 of AI technology spend. Most UK SMEs underspend this line dramatically.

The headline mistake is to compare a £25 per user per month tool against a £150,000 bespoke deployment as if they were the same kind of investment. They are not. Per-seat tools deliver function-level uplift if used and almost nothing if ignored. Bespoke deployments deliver enterprise-level uplift if integrated, redesigned around and adopted, and a writedown if not.

How long is the payback period?

The published payback ranges for the three categories above:

  1. Per-seat productivity AI: 3 to 9 months in well-adopted teams. Payback depends almost entirely on whether usage is real. UK firms tracking active-user rates rather than licence counts tend to land at the lower end. Firms that buy seats and never measure usage rarely see payback at all.
  2. Off-the-shelf bots and assistants for specific functions: 6 to 18 months. The wide range reflects how integration-dependent these are. Where the bot connects to clean data and a clear workflow, payback is fast. Where it sits on top of legacy data with conflicting sources, it is slow.
  3. Bespoke deployments and custom agents: 12 to 36 months. The function delivers ROI in year one (sometimes year two). Enterprise EBIT impact, where it appears, lands in years two and three.

One important counter-narrative finding worth holding in mind. The Metr study of experienced software developers, summarised by MIT's Initiative on the Digital Economy, found that participants self-reported a 20% productivity uplift from AI coding tools but were measured to be 20% slower on the actual task completion. Self-reported productivity is not productivity. Any UK leader signing off an AI investment should require objective measurement, not survey questions to staff about how much faster they feel.

How should a UK leader measure AI ROI honestly?

Five practical actions for a UK leader assessing or running AI investment in 2026.

  1. Measure outcomes, not adoption. Track active usage by individual, completion rates on the workflows AI is meant to support, error rates, and customer or staff satisfaction. Licences purchased are a vanity metric.
  2. Separate function-level claims from enterprise-level claims. If your business case promises enterprise EBIT impact, ask which of the published 6% you expect to be in. If your business case is a function-level productivity claim, present it as such and protect it from over-promising.
  3. Test for measurement honesty. If your only ROI evidence is staff reporting they feel more productive, build in objective measurement before scaling. The Metr finding is the canonical warning.
  4. Budget the people line at scale. McKinsey's £5-to-£1 ratio is a useful upper bound, but even half that level is more than most UK SMEs have allocated. Training, role redesign, and change management are the difference between function-level wins and enterprise impact.
  5. Set a 12-month review gate. Set the criteria for continuing, expanding or stopping each AI investment before you start. Review against the BCC adoption-versus-impact gap honestly: 95% of UK firms using AI saw no headcount change in the past 12 months; if your case promised one, the absence is information.

The honest 2026 position for a UK business is that AI ROI is available but it is not free. The published evidence rewards firms that adopt deliberately, measure objectively, invest in people alongside technology, and accept that enterprise-level impact is rare and earned. Firms that buy licences and hope for the best are paying for a productivity uplift their staff feel and do not get.
