Before you sign, get written answers on five things: how UK GDPR Article 28 responsibilities are split, whether your data trains the vendor's model, who carries the liability if the system counts as high-risk under the EU AI Act, how the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025's automated-decision rules apply (in force since 5 February 2026), and what the exit and audit rights actually say. No answer, no signature.

When do you need an AI vendor contract checklist?

Any AI vendor contract needs this checklist before signature, not after. That covers a new generative AI subscription, an AI feature bolted onto software you already use, or a custom-built model built for one client. The ICO's AI toolkit is explicit that due diligence checks on an AI provider need to happen at procurement stage, alongside a data protection impact assessment where the processing carries real risk.

Picture a 40-person UK retailer buying a customer-service chatbot licence. The due diligence it needs is the same in kind as a public body procuring a case-management tool, just smaller in scale. Public buyers already have to ask suppliers to disclose AI use in a tender response under the Cabinet Office's own transparency rules (PPN 017, Improving transparency of AI use in procurement). A private UK SME won't get that disclosure for free. It has to ask.

What should an AI vendor contract checklist actually cover?

Four categories do most of the work: data protection, AI-specific regulation, liability and audit rights, and the commercial terms that determine what happens when the relationship ends or the model changes underneath you.

Data protection and UK GDPR

  1. Who is the controller and who is the processor? Get this in writing before anything else. Most AI vendor relationships are controller-processor, but some tools (particularly ones that improve their model from your usage) can tip the vendor into joint controller territory, which changes the whole liability picture.
  2. Does the contract include the Article 28 mandatory terms? UK GDPR requires the subject matter, duration, nature and purpose of the processing, the type of personal data, the categories of data subjects, and the controller's instructions to be set out explicitly, alongside confidentiality, sub-processor authorisation, deletion or return of data, and audit rights (ICO, contracts and liabilities guidance). A vendor's standard terms rarely cover all of it.
  3. Is your data used to train or improve the vendor's model? Ask this one directly and get the answer in the contract, not in a sales call. If the vendor uses customer inputs to train models outside your own tenant, you need an explicit opt-out clause, not a policy page that can change unilaterally.
  4. What changes if the tool makes decisions about people? Since 5 February 2026, the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 replaced UK GDPR Article 22 with a permission-based regime for automated decision-making (ICO, DUAA guidance for organisations). Where a decision is significant and based on special category data, the vendor needs to support the required safeguards: information for the person affected, a way to challenge it, and human intervention on request.

EU AI Act and other UK AI regulation

  1. Does this count as a high-risk AI system under the EU AI Act? If the answer is yes and the business operates in or sells into the EU, deployer obligations under Article 26 apply from 2 August 2026, including retaining the system's automatically generated logs for at least six months (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689).
  2. Which obligations sit with the vendor, and which sit with you? The provider's own conformity assessment covers the system as designed. It doesn't cover how your business deploys it, what data you feed it, or whether your staff are competent to oversee it. If the contract is silent on the split, that silence is the gap.
  3. Who carries the liability if a regulator fines the business over this system? Tom & Co's analysis of the AI Act's penalty tiers puts the break-even turnover at roughly £420 million, above which the fixed euro cap rather than the percentage-of-turnover cap applies. Most UK SMEs sit well under that figure. Know which cap applies to your business before agreeing to one.
  4. Which UK regulator would look at this if something went wrong? The answer depends on the sector. A vendor that can't tell you which UK regulator (the ICO, the FCA, the SRA, or another) would take an interest hasn't thought through your compliance position, only its own.

Liability, IP and audit rights

  1. Does the vendor warrant that its training data was lawfully sourced? Ask for an intellectual property indemnity that covers claims arising from the training data, not just from your own use of the output.
  2. Do you have the right to audit or request evidence of compliance? The ICO's AI toolkit recommends contracts include audit clauses and that buyers request algorithmic impact assessment reports covering bias, accuracy and the trade-offs made in the system's design. A vendor that resists this is telling you something.
  3. What accuracy or bias standard is contractually guaranteed? Decide the acceptable level before you sign, then get it written in as a key performance indicator or service-level term, not left as a marketing claim. The Crown Commercial Service's own Model Services Contract buyer guidance builds named IP, data protection and liability terms into every AI-related public sector deal, a reasonable benchmark for what a private contract should contain too.
  4. What happens if the vendor changes the underlying model? A silent swap from one model version to another can change accuracy, tone and even data handling overnight. Ask for advance notice of material model changes and the right to test before they go live.

Commercial terms and exit rights

  1. What is the data return and deletion process at contract end? Specify the format and the timeframe, not just "on request".
  2. Is pricing tied to usage that could spike unpredictably? Token-based and per-query pricing can move a lot faster than a fixed-seat licence. Get a cap or an alert threshold written in.
  3. What termination rights exist if the vendor's compliance status changes? If the vendor loses a certification, fails an audit, or gets a regulatory enforcement notice, you want an exit route that doesn't depend on proving breach in court first.

How is an AI vendor contract different from a standard software contract?

A standard SaaS agreement covers most of the commercial basics well. What it usually misses is anything specific to how an AI system behaves once it's live, which is exactly where the risk sits.

How do you use this checklist during procurement?

Three steps turn this from a list into a process.

1. Send it as a written request for information before you sign anything. Put the data protection and AI Act questions in writing and ask for written answers, not a call with the sales team. A vendor's willingness to answer in writing tells you almost as much as the answers themselves.

2. Treat a vague or missing answer as a stop condition, not a formality. "We take data protection seriously" is not an answer to "is our data used to train your model". If a vendor won't commit to specifics, that's the finding, not a gap to fill in later.

3. Get legal sign-off against a short list of non-negotiables. For most UK SMEs that's the training-data opt-out, a clear split of EU AI Act obligations, and audit rights. Everything else is a negotiation. Those three usually aren't.

What do you do with the vendor's answers?

Some answers should stop the process outright. No opt-out from model training on your data, no willingness to name which party carries AI Act obligations, or a flat refusal of any audit right are all reasons to go back to legal before going any further, whatever the sales timeline says.

Other gaps are negotiable rather than fatal. A vendor that hasn't thought about log retention or hasn't set an accuracy KPI yet can often be brought to a reasonable position with a contract amendment, particularly if the business is small enough that you're not its only customer asking.

Keep the completed checklist on file once the contract is signed. It becomes the evidence a data protection impact assessment or an ICO enquiry will ask for later, and it's far cheaper to have answered these questions once at procurement than to reconstruct them under pressure after something has gone wrong.
