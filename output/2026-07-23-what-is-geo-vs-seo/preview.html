What is GEO and how is it different from SEO?

GEO (Generative Engine Optimization) is the practice of structuring content so AI systems like ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity and Google's AI Overviews cite it directly in their answers, rather than just ranking it in a list of blue links. Where SEO chases position on a results page, GEO chases being the sentence an AI quotes. Princeton's 2024 GEO study found the right techniques lift citation likelihood by up to 40%.

What does GEO actually mean?

GEO stands for Generative Engine Optimization, a term introduced in a 2024 paper by Princeton University researchers, presented at KDD, one of the top data mining conferences in the world. The idea is straightforward: optimise content so a generative AI system chooses to cite it, not just so a search engine ranks it on a page.

Generative engines are the AI systems increasingly standing between your customer and your website: ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Gemini, and Google's AI Overviews. Instead of returning ten blue links, they read across dozens of pages and write a direct answer, sometimes with a citation, sometimes without. GEO is the discipline of making your content the one that gets cited.

Picture a Manchester-based accountancy practice searching Perplexity for "best payroll software for a 20-person UK firm". Perplexity doesn't show ten results. It writes three paragraphs and names two or three vendors, each with a small citation link. GEO decides whether your client's website is one of those links, or invisible.

The name overlaps with a few others floating around marketing teams right now, AEO (answer engine optimisation) and AIO (AI optimisation) are used almost interchangeably. GEO is the term that stuck in the academic literature, so it's the one worth learning if you only learn one.

None of this is about tricking an AI model into citing you. The same techniques (specificity, sourcing, structure) that lift GEO performance also make content more useful to a human reader stumbling on the page directly, which is the whole point of writing it well in the first place.

How is GEO different from traditional SEO?

SEO and GEO share a family resemblance, both want your content found, both reward clarity and authority, but they optimise for different judges. SEO plays to a ranking algorithm sorting a list. GEO plays to a language model synthesising an answer and deciding which sources earned a mention.

The clearest way to see the split is side by side.

DimensionSEOGEO
Primary goalRank in the top 10 blue linksGet cited or quoted inside the AI's written answer
Target surfaceGoogle and Bing search results pagesChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Gemini, Google AI Overviews
What winsBacklinks, keyword match, page speed, domain authoritySpecific stats with dates, quotable definitions, clear structure
How you're measuredPosition (rank 1 to 10), organic click-through rateCitation frequency, share of voice inside AI answers
Content that performsLong, keyword-optimised pages built for scanningDirect 40 to 80 word answers, dated statistics, named sources
Time to see results3 to 6 months for a competitive termDays to weeks once a generative engine re-crawls

Note the last row. SEO rewards patience. GEO can shift within days of a generative engine's next crawl and re-synthesis, which is exactly why so few UK marketing teams have adjusted their content yet, and exactly why the ones who do have an open runway.

The skills overlap more than the tooling suggests. A content team that already writes clearly, cites sources and structures pages well is most of the way to strong GEO performance. What changes is the emphasis: fewer keyword-stuffed paragraphs, more citable, standalone facts.

Why does GEO matter for UK businesses right now?

The audience has already moved. According to Ofcom's Adults' Media Use and Attitudes 2026 report, published 2 April 2026, 75% of UK online adults now read AI-generated search summaries at least sometimes, and 54% say they use AI tools at all, up from 31% just a year earlier.

On the business side, the British Chambers of Commerce and University of Essex's AI survey, published March 2026 with Atos, found 54% of UK SMEs now use AI in some form, up from 35% in 2025, 25% in 2024 and 23% in 2023. Marketing is the single most common use case, tied with admin at 72%.

Tom & Co analysis: combining the BCC and University of Essex's 2026 figures (54% of UK SMEs using AI, 72% of those using it for marketing) with gov.uk's Business Population Estimates 2024 (5,499,000 UK private-sector businesses) suggests roughly 2.1 million UK businesses now use AI for marketing or content work. Most of them have not yet adjusted a single page for how a generative engine reads it.

Retail adds another data point. ONS retail sales figures for 2026 show internet sales holding at 27 to 28% of all UK retail throughout the year. A growing share of that traffic no longer starts with a click on a blue link, it starts with an AI-written summary of what to buy and where.

Ignore it and the cost isn't a ranking drop, it's invisibility inside a growing share of buying decisions. When a generative engine names three vendors and yours isn't one of them, a prospect may never see your website at all, however well it ranks on page one of Google.

Who's already doing this, and who's ignoring it?

The gap is still wide open. Most UK marketing teams have spent a decade optimising for Google's blue links and haven't touched a page's structure for a chat-based answer engine. That's not a criticism, it's an opportunity: a market this early rewards whoever moves first, the same way early schema markup adopters had an edge over rivals a decade ago.

Early movement tends to cluster where the stakes and budgets are highest. UK professional services firms (accountants, solicitors, financial advisers) and ecommerce brands with genuine price and stock data to publish are furthest ahead, because they already had structured data to draw on. Smaller service businesses, this article's likely reader, are mostly still writing SEO-era prose.

Two competing UK accountancy practices might publish near-identical service pages. If only one has a 60-word definitive answer, a dated fee benchmark and a real comparison table, that's the one Perplexity or ChatGPT is more likely to name when comparing which LLM to trust for business advice, and the same logic holds when a prospect just asks which accountant to use.

What actually improves your GEO score?

The Princeton team didn't just name the phenomenon, they tested a range of specific writing interventions against real generative engine outputs and measured which ones actually shifted citation rates. Three matter most for a UK marketing team drafting its next page.

The GEO-bench framework behind those figures tested content across roughly ten thousand real queries spanning nine different domains, from historical facts to product recommendations, which is why the findings translate reasonably well to a UK business's own content rather than staying a lab curiosity.

Cite authoritative sources and use direct quotations

Adding a quotation from a credible source lifted citation likelihood by up to 40% in the Princeton GEO study, the single biggest lever they tested. In practice: quote the regulator, quote the named survey, don't paraphrase it into vague prose.

Back every claim with a specific, dated statistic

Statistics with units and dates attached (not "many businesses", but "54% of UK SMEs, March 2026") lifted citation rates by roughly 37% in the same study. A vague claim gives a generative engine nothing to quote. A dated figure gives it a sentence to lift verbatim.

Structure the page so an AI can extract it cleanly

Generative engines favour content with a clear, self-contained answer near the top, question-format headings, and genuine tables over prose trying to describe the same comparison. Schema markup helps too. It doesn't guarantee a citation, but it removes the guesswork of what your page is actually about.

Where does GEO fit alongside your existing SEO work?

GEO doesn't replace SEO, it sits alongside it. A page still needs to rank to get crawled in the first place, and most generative engines are still pulling heavily from pages that already do well in the same retrieval-augmented generation process that lets an AI assistant pull in outside content. Think of SEO as earning the invitation and GEO as what you say once you're in the room.

A Leeds-based B2B software vendor doesn't need a separate content team for GEO. The same page that ranks for "best UK payroll software" can be rewritten with a tighter 60-word answer up top, a comparison table instead of three paragraphs of prose, and one dated stat, and it will do both jobs at once.

How do you measure whether GEO is working?

Traditional rank trackers don't see AI answers, so you need a different habit, not necessarily a different tool budget. Three checks work without any paid software, and they tell you more about the ROI of AI work like a content rewrite than a rankings dashboard ever will.

  • Ask the question yourself, regularly. Put your own headline query into ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity and Google's AI mode once a month and note whether your business gets named.
  • Check your server logs for AI crawler visits. GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot and Google-Extended requests are a leading indicator that a page is even in the running.
  • Watch referral traffic from AI platforms. Most analytics tools now separate ChatGPT, Perplexity and Copilot referrals from standard search traffic, even if the volume starts small.

A small category of GEO tracking tools (Profound and others) now automate the manual prompting exercise above, running your target queries across multiple AI platforms daily and reporting citation share the way a rank tracker reports position. Worth evaluating once you've proven the manual version is worth the time.

Imagine running this check for a retail client and finding your brand named in AI answers for two of five target queries within six weeks of rewriting just the top pages. That's the kind of pace this loop can move at once the content itself changes, far faster than a typical SEO campaign converts into ranking movement.

None of these numbers behave like a traditional SEO dashboard yet, and that's fine. Early, noisy data beats no data while the category is still forming.

What should a UK marketing team do this quarter?

Four actions, in order of effort. None of them need new headcount or a big budget, just an hour a week and a willingness to rewrite pages that already exist, rather than commissioning new ones.

  • Audit your top 10 pages for a citable answer paragraph. If a reader, or an AI, can't find a 40 to 80 word direct answer near the top, add one.
  • Replace one vague claim per page with a dated statistic. Pull it from a primary UK source, not a rival's blog.
  • Turn your best prose comparison into a real table. Rows, columns, actual data in every cell.
  • Check whether your robots.txt allows the AI crawlers. GPTBot, Google-Extended, PerplexityBot, ClaudeBot and CCBot all need explicit permission, or none of the above matters.

None of this requires a rebuild. It requires picking one page a week and asking whether an AI reading it cold would have anything worth quoting.
